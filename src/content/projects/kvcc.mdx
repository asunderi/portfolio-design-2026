## Overview

Some places shape you early and stay with you. Kalamazoo Valley Community College was that place for me — first as an intern in 2011, then as a full member of the web team nearly a decade later. By the time I returned in 2019, I understood the institution, its people, and its constraints in a way that let me hit the ground running and contribute meaningfully from day one.

Over three years I worked on a small, skilled team of five — each person owning their own projects and areas of expertise. My work spanned internal applications, public-facing web design, and a large-scale event platform, culminating in three projects that each left a lasting mark on how the college operated.

Joining an established web team mid-stride requires a certain kind of adaptability. His institutional knowledge — the infrastructure, the constraints, the unwritten rules of how things worked — helped me settle into my own role quickly and confidently. ## Staff Development

For years, the idea of a dedicated staff development platform at KVCC had stalled. Previous attempts had never gained enough momentum — there was always something else, always competing priorities, never quite enough dedicated time to see it through.

What changed was Summit.

When the decision was made to expand Summit — the college's bi-annual professional development event — from faculty only to all staff, the need for a proper internal system became urgent. An organization-wide event of that scale needed infrastructure that worked exactly as required. That urgency finally brought Staff Development to life.

### What I Built

Staff Development became a robust internal web application allowing employees to browse, register for, and track onboarding sessions, training, and enrichment programs. The platform needed to handle:

- Complex user definitions and authentication tiers
- Large volumes of content across multiple departments
- Administrative event management for staff running the programs

Working in Laravel for the first time, I took the time to properly learn the framework's documentation rather than cutting corners. The result was a scalable, flexible application — one that could grow with the institution's needs rather than requiring a rebuild every time requirements changed.

## Summit

Summit had always been a significant event. Expanding it to include all college employees made it something else entirely — effectively closing the institution for nearly two business days to allow for organization-wide professional development. The implications were massive, and the platform had to match that scale.

### What I Built

Built as a standalone web application layered on top of the Staff Development infrastructure, Summit allowed users to log in, browse simultaneous schedules across multiple tracks, and register for specific breakout sessions — all in real time.

The work required close collaboration with multiple departments to ensure content accuracy and scheduling integrity. On-the-fly modifications during the event itself were inevitable, and the system had to handle them gracefully.

The result was a platform with a strong event identity, improved faculty and employee engagement, and the flexibility to evolve with each successive Summit.

## Getting Started

Getting Started was a different kind of challenge — not a complex application, but a public-facing resource designed to help prospective students navigate the beginning of their journey with the Enrollment department.

I entered the project close to launch, which meant working quickly and largely independently. Marketing had been developing a companion print document in parallel — by the time I was aware of it, it was nearly at print. Most of what I designed for Getting Started I shaped on my own, with light collaboration from my immediate team.

### What I Built

A suite of web guides breaking down complex enrollment processes into clear, approachable steps. The work focused on reducing overwhelm — careful attention to layout and hierarchy so that students of all backgrounds could quickly find what they needed and move forward with confidence.

The results spoke for themselves: an improved enrollment experience, a dedicated digital enrollment footprint, and the integration of 3D campus tours that brought the college to life for students who hadn't yet set foot on campus.

Working with the Enrollment department was a genuine pleasure. They were communicative, clear about what they needed, and deeply invested in the outcome. Being able to bring a department's vision to life through design is something I find deeply satisfying — and this project was one of the cleaner examples of that collaboration working exactly as it should.

## Reflection

KVCC was where I learned what it means to be a designer inside an institution — navigating interdepartmental relationships, working within brand constraints, building things that real people rely on every day. The work was rarely glamorous, but it was meaningful. Every project touched students, faculty, or staff in some direct way.

Returning as a full team member after my internship years earlier gave me a perspective I wouldn't trade. I understood where things came from, which made it easier to know where to push and where to hold steady. That kind of institutional empathy is something you can't shortcut — and it made me a better collaborator and a more considered designer.
