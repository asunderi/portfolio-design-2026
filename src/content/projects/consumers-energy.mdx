## Overview

When a major utility company needs to assess damage across a wide service area — think storms, outages, infrastructure events — the teams responding need information fast, clearly organized, and accessible in the field. Before this project, Consumers Energy had no dedicated tool built for that purpose.

I joined as the UI/UX designer on a contract team tasked with designing and building the first damage assessment application purpose-built for their operations — a platform that would serve field workers, team leads, and office staff simultaneously, pulling data from multiple sources into a single coherent interface.

---

## The Challenge

Damage assessment at the scale of a utility company is a genuinely complex operational problem. The app needed to serve four distinct user types across very different working conditions:

- **Field workers** assessing damage on the ground, often on tablets in unpredictable environments
- **Team leads** dispatching and coordinating crews across affected areas
- **Office staff** managing incoming data, documentation, and reporting
- **Management and leadership** reviewing outcomes and response efficiency

Each persona had different needs, different technical comfort levels, and different physical contexts for using the app. Designing a single system that worked across all of them — without overwhelming any one group — required careful user journey mapping and a clear information hierarchy.

---

## The Process

I began by mapping user journeys and workflows in Figma, using storyboards to visualize how each persona moved through the system. This phase was as much about aligning the team and stakeholders on how damage assessment actually worked as it was about design — making implicit processes explicit so we could design for them deliberately.

The storyboarding approach proved especially valuable for a system with this many interconnected workflows. Before a single screen was designed, everyone involved had a shared picture of what we were building and why.

From there I moved into prototyping, designing UI components and interaction patterns that could handle large volumes of data without losing clarity. Ongoing collaboration with stakeholders and developers shaped each iteration, ensuring the designs stayed grounded in how the app would actually be used.

---

## The Power Apps Constraint

The platform decision — Microsoft Power Apps — had been made before I joined. My job was to design within it, not around it.

Power Apps presents a specific challenge for UI/UX work: it is not built for responsive viewports. The platform is effectively designed for tablet use in landscape or portrait orientation, and it handles data-heavy interfaces by dumping content from tables — which are notoriously difficult to make responsive or visually digestible.

This constraint forced a different kind of design thinking. Rather than starting from an ideal interface and adapting it to the platform, I had to understand the platform's limitations first and design upward from there. That meant finding ways to present complex, multi-source data clearly within a rigid framework — prioritizing information hierarchy, visual grouping, and interaction patterns that worked within what Power Apps could actually do.

It was a meaningful lesson in the difference between designing for a blank canvas and designing for a constrained system. The latter requires more discipline and, ultimately, produces more durable solutions.

---

## The Result

During my time on the project, the application was put through its first major real-world tests — one or two significant damage events that ran through the system while it was still in active development. The response from team leads and leadership was positive. For the first time, field data, dispatch coordination, and office reporting were flowing through a single dedicated tool rather than being gathered and reconciled across disconnected sources.

The project was still in active development with major features remaining when my contract concluded. But the foundation — the user journeys, the interaction model, the design system within Power Apps — was in place.

---

## Reflection

Consumers Energy pushed me into unfamiliar territory in useful ways. Designing for enterprise-scale operational software is different from designing for consumer products — the stakes are higher, the user contexts are more demanding, and the data is less forgiving. Working within Power Apps reinforced something I now carry into every project: constraints are not obstacles to good design, they are the conditions within which good design happens.

Understanding the platform before touching a single screen would have saved rework. Understanding it deeply made the final work stronger.
