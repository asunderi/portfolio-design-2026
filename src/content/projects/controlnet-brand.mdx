## Overview

A brand that hadn't been meaningfully updated in over a decade. Previous redesign attempts that hadn't made it through approval. And a company expanding into new cities just as the work began.

The ControlNET rebrand wasn't a quick refresh — it was a four-year process of building a cohesive visual identity from the ground up and rolling it out across every surface the organization touched.

---

## The Starting Point

When I joined ControlNET in 2015, the existing logo was plain but not broken — a mark that had served its purpose for over ten years without much thought given to where the company was heading. The brand colors were red and blue, functional for the industry but limited in what they could communicate as the business evolved.

Previous attempts at a redesign had failed to gain approval. That history mattered — it meant any new direction needed to be genuinely considered, well-presented, and easy for leadership to see themselves in.

---

## The Logo

The design challenge wasn't just aesthetic — it was meaningful. ControlNET was opening a new location, expanding its regional footprint at the same moment the rebrand was beginning. That timing became the conceptual foundation for the new mark.

Rather than a generic corporate symbol, I designed a custom skyline incorporating recognizable architectural silhouettes from the cities where ControlNET operated. The result was a logo that was distinctly theirs — rooted in place, reflective of growth, and immediately differentiated from anything else in the controls industry.

The process involved multiple concepts and iterations, all of which were presented to the general manager and reviewed in close collaboration with the company president. Getting to approval required patience, clear rationale, and a willingness to explore directions that didn't always feel like the right answer — until one did.

## The Color Palette

The original palette — red and blue — stayed. Both colors carried weight in the industry: blue for trust and reliability, red for energy and attention. Rather than replacing them, I extended the palette with the addition of a bright green.

The reasoning was strategic, not decorative. ControlNET was actively expanding into energy-related certifications, and green gave the brand a way to signal that dimension of the business visually. It also brought the palette to life — shifting it from a flat, utilitarian combination into something with more range and personality.

## The Rollout

A rebrand is only as strong as its implementation. Over the course of nearly four years, the new identity touched nearly every aspect of the organization:

- **Vehicles** — fleet graphics updated to the new mark and palette
- **Uniforms** — employee workwear reflecting the refreshed identity
- **Digital** — website redesigned within the new brand system
- **Print & Collateral** — letterheads, business cards, proposals, forms, and marketing materials (covered in depth in the Print & Collateral case study)
- **Signage** — new office locations erected signage under the new brand

The scope of the rollout meant the brand had to be genuinely flexible — holding up across large vehicle graphics and small business card text, in full color and single ink, on screen and in print.

## Reflection

This was my longest single brand engagement, and the one that taught me the most about what brand identity really means. A logo is a starting point — the real work is building something consistent enough to survive years of application across an entire organization, by people who weren't part of the original design decisions.

The ControlNET rebrand also taught me the value of persistence and clear communication with stakeholders. Previous attempts had failed not because of design quality, but because the right ideas hadn't been framed in the right way. Getting a rebrand approved and implemented across an organization requires as much strategic thinking as creative.
