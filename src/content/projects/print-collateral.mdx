## Overview

Print design demands a different kind of thinking. There's no browser to render a fallback, no hover state to clarify intent, no update to push after launch. What goes to production is what people hold, read, and experience in the real world.

Across two very different industries — digital controls and video games — I designed and produced over 50 print pieces ranging from single-color mailed envelopes to large-format convention banners seen by thousands. Each project pushed me to think beyond the screen and design with material, production, and physical context in mind.

---

## ControlNET — Brand Collateral Redesign

When I joined ControlNET, the brand hadn't been updated in nearly a decade. My work wasn't just to refresh a few templates — it was to build a cohesive print identity from the ground up, across every touchpoint the company used.

### What I Designed

The scope covered the full range of a business's printed life:

- **Brand foundation** — logo redesign, updated color palette, brand guidelines
- **Corporate materials** — letterheads, envelopes, business cards
- **Client-facing documents** — proposals for large HVAC bids, service agreements
- **Operational forms** — material purchase orders, installation checklists, fillable PDFs
- **Marketing** — convention banners, car decals, event materials including menu cards

### The Single-Color Challenge

One of the most instructive constraints was designing for single-color envelope printing. Most of my experience to that point had been digital — full color, full bleed, no limits. Envelopes are different. A single-color print means every design decision has to work in one ink, no gradients, no color contrast to lean on.

This pushed me to think more carefully about shape, weight, and negative space. The logo redesign had to hold up at small sizes in a single color — a constraint that ultimately made it stronger across all formats.

### Scale

Across my time at ControlNET I produced dozens of pieces — internal forms, client proposals, marketing materials, and event collateral — building and maintaining a consistent brand identity across all of them.

## S2 Games — PAX Prime 2014

PAX Prime is one of the largest gaming conventions in North America. When S2 Games attended in 2014 to promote Strife, I was responsible for designing the full suite of print and large-format materials representing the game at the event.

### What I Designed

- **Large format** — building awning banners, billboard graphics, lightning banners, kiosk banners
- **Convention display** — booth graphics and display materials
- **Event handouts** — flyers, table tents, take-home materials
- **Supporting digital** — cosplay competition website tied to the event campaign

### First Large Format Experience

PAX Prime was my introduction to large-format print production. Designing something that will be printed at billboard or building-banner scale is a fundamentally different exercise than screen design. Pixel density, viewing distance, color profiles for print — all of it had to be learned and applied under a real deadline for a real event.

Working closely with the Marketing department, I made sure every piece felt cohesive with Strife's visual identity while standing out in the sensory-heavy environment of a major convention floor.

### The Context

Convention design has one job: stop people in a crowd. The materials had to communicate the game's personality instantly, from across a busy exhibition hall. That clarity of purpose — design for a single moment of attention — is something I carry into every project since.

## What Print Taught Me

Working across both of these engagements deepened my respect for constraints. Digital design is forgiving — print is not. Every decision carries weight because it's permanent once it goes to production.

Designing for single-color envelopes, building-scale banners, fillable PDFs, and event handouts all in the same career chapter gave me a foundation that makes me a more careful, considered designer — in print and on screen.
